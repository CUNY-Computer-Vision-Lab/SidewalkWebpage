Surface Problems

Little blurb on what a surface problem is

How should I label sidewalk cracks?

Only label sidewalk cracks that would result in a bumpy or otherwise uncomfortable experience for someone using a wheelchair or other assistive device. Small elevation changes will typically be noted as severity 1, as seen in the example below, provided there are no other issues with the surface. Follow a similar procedure for sidewalk cracks that come from weathering; only label them if they would cause difficult traversal.

How should I label driveways?

DO place a label (on each side of the driveway) if a driveway causes a steep slope in the sidewalk (ADA 403.3). This illustration is bad enough to warrant a severity of ~4, since it could greatly destabilize a user of a wheelchair.

DO NOT place any labels if the driveway is level with the sidewalk, as in the examples below.

How should I label gravel?

DO label gravel surfaces, as they are not firm/stable enough for a wheelchair/walker (ADA 302.1). They typically get a severity of ~5 since they are so hard to traverse by wheelchair.

How should I label cobblestone and other rough surfaces?

DO label cobblestone and other rough surfaces, since they can be difficult/painful to traverse due to the vibrations and catching of wheelchair casters (USAB ADA guidelines 302.1). In addition, as shared by one of our wheelchair pilot testers, these types of surfaces can also make it difficult to control the joystick in electric chairs. The example below would get a severity rating of ~3, though cobblestone with wider/narrower gaps may have higher/lower severity.

How should I label grates, gaps, and other openings?

DO label grates, gaps, and other opening if (and only if) it appears that a wheelchair caster could get caught or if the ride would be extremely bumpy (ADA 302.3). (The ADA specifies ½” openings but do your best to estimate). For grates, pay particular attention to whether the elongated openings are perpendicular to the direction of travel.

Severity will be higher if the grates are not perpendicular to the direction of travel. Severity will be also higher for wider gaps, and it will be higher for grates that cover more of the sidewalk (i.e., if the grating is easy to avoid, it gets a lower severity, see avoidable problems).

How should I label narrow sidewalks?

DO label if a long stretch of sidewalk is less than 3ft wide (ADA 403.5). Place one label at the beginning of a stretch of narrow sidewalk, and one label at the end of that stretch. If the narrow sidewalk stretches multiple streets, label at the beginning and end of each street (with only 1 needed at a corner).

DO NOT label if the stretch of narrow sidewalk is very short, as often occurs to get around trees (unless the narrow section is very narrow) (ADA 403.5.1).

How should I label debris on sidewalk?

DO label if there are a large amount of dead leaves (or other debris) on the ground (FHWA Guide for Maintaining Pedestrian Facilities for Enhanced Safety 3.2.2). This could cause slippery conditions or could slow down wheelchair users in extreme cases. Label as temporary.

How should I label grass on sidewalk blocks?

DO label these as surface problems unless they are very minor; however, they are typically only severity of 1 or 2 (unless extreme). Though they may not always directly impact the accessibility of the sidewalk, they are “surface problems”. Without being monitored, they could deteriorate further and cause accessibility problems in the future (FHWA Guide for Maintaining Pedestrian Facilities for Enhanced Safety 3.2.1). Whether or not to label these is difficult to assess. On the left, there are probably two surface problem labels to be applied. On the right, it is very ambiguous, but there are two that should probably be labeled, with further ones down possibly being labeled, though closer inspection would be necessary.

DO label surface problems, even if they are avoidable. In the cases in which they are avoidable, as in the picture on the right, you can mark a lower severity rating (this one having severity ~2).
