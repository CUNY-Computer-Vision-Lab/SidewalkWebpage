Corners with only one curb ramp

DO place a missing curb ramp if the existing ramp is clearly only meant for crossing one of the streets. For example, in the image on left, the arrow marks a missing curb ramp because there is a crosswalk but no curb ramp and the existing ramp is facing the other street. This would get a severity rating ~3 because there is another curb ramp on that corner that could be used instead, though pedestrians are moderately lead into traffic due to it’s orientation (ADA 406.6).

DO NOT place a missing curb ramp label if the one curb ramp is clearly meant for crossing both streets (as in image on right), but the severity of the curb ramp is likely > 1, depending on how much it would lead someone into traffic (ADA 406.6). In the example below on the right, the severity rating would be ~2, since pedestrians are minorly directed into traffic. It would have a severity of 3-4 if the visible lanes were not parking lanes.

Where there is NO sidewalk, but there IS a curb ramp

DO place a curb ramp label if there is a curb ramp, regardless of the presence of sidewalk (or the usefulness of the curb ramp. A curb ramp is a curb ramp is a curb ramp is a curb ramp! In the image on the left, the curb ramps could be useful for someone who wants to cross both streets. They could safely cross one street, then safely wait on the space provided by the curb ramps for an opportune time to cross the other street. The image on the left… No idea why it is there, but place a label anyway.

Where there is NO sidewalk and there is NO curb ramp

DO NOT place a missing curb ramp if there is no sidewalk. A curb ramp is not required in these situations (nor would it be very useful) since this is not part of a pedestrian route. Do not place a missing curb ramp even in those cases where there are crosswalks but no sidewalk as in the image on the right (ADA 303.4)
