Obstacles Overview

Background on obstacles. The ADA standards require a “clear floor or ground space” along accessible pedestrian routes (ADA 305). This allows pedestrians to remain safely on the sidewalk or crosswalk; clear paths are particularly important for pedestrians using walkers or wheelchairs, since moving off the path to avoid an obstacle may be impossible or more likely to cause imbalance, tripping, etc.

What should I mark as an obstacle? Place an obstacle label on any object that is directly on the path of a pedestrian route (meaning, do not place labels on objects that do not block a path).

How should I rate severity? Severity is higher for obstacles that cover more of the path, have less room for navigating around them, or would cause pedestrians that avoid them to put themselves into harm’s way (by going into traffic, going off the sidewalk, etc). There are numerous examples below of permanent and temporary obstacles at a range of severity ratings.

Important note on label placement: Place labels at the base of obstacles. Placing at higher positions may increase the likelihood that lat-lng estimation from the label is inaccurate. Notice on the left below, how the label that is not placed at the base of the tree has a lat-lng that it is very far from the sidewalk (seen in the mini map).

Important note on labeling a long stretch of construction: If a long stretch of sidewalk is under construction (as in the example on the left below), place a temporary obstacle label once at the beginning of the construction and once at the end, in the same manner as in the Surface problems section.

Permanent Obstacles

Any permanent fixture that is placed in the direct path of pedestrian routes is a permanent obstacle. This could also include extreme instances of overgrowth, as in the fourth example. Poles that are located (even partially) within curb ramp flares are an edge case, but you should mark them as obstacles.

Temporary Obstacles

Generally encompasses obstacles that are not part of the “built environment”, such as construction, minor overgrowth, parked cars, and trash cans.

Non-obstacles

Any objects that are not directly on the path of a pedestrian route (as in the first three examples below) should not be marked as obstacles. Perhaps more obviously, things like moving cars and other pedestrians are not obstacles either.
