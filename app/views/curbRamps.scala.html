Corners with only one curb ramp

DO place a missing curb ramp if the existing ramp is clearly only meant for crossing one of the streets. For example, in the image on left, the arrow marks a missing curb ramp because there is a crosswalk but no curb ramp and the existing ramp is facing the other street. This would get a severity rating ~3 because there is another curb ramp on that corner that could be used instead, though pedestrians are moderately lead into traffic due to it’s orientation (ADA 406.6).

DO NOT place a missing curb ramp label if the one curb ramp is clearly meant for crossing both streets (as in image on right), but the severity of the curb ramp is likely > 1, depending on how much it would lead someone into traffic (ADA 406.6). In the example below on the right, the severity rating would be ~2, since pedestrians are minorly directed into traffic. It would have a severity of 3-4 if the visible lanes were not parking lanes.

Where there is NO sidewalk, but there IS a curb ramp

DO place a curb ramp label if there is a curb ramp, regardless of the presence of sidewalk (or the usefulness of the curb ramp. A curb ramp is a curb ramp is a curb ramp is a curb ramp! In the image on the left, the curb ramps could be useful for someone who wants to cross both streets. They could safely cross one street, then safely wait on the space provided by the curb ramps for an opportune time to cross the other street. The image on the left… No idea why it is there, but place a label anyway.

Where there is NO sidewalk and there is NO curb ramp

DO NOT place a missing curb ramp if there is no sidewalk. A curb ramp is not required in these situations (nor would it be very useful) since this is not part of a pedestrian route. Do not place a missing curb ramp even in those cases where there are crosswalks but no sidewalk as in the image on the right (ADA 303.4)

Driveways

DO place a surface problem label (note: not a ‘missing curb ramp’ label) if the driveway is not level with the sidewalk (there is a steep slope) as in the illustration on the left. Because this is a surface problem and not a curb ramp problem, we cover this in more detail in the Surface Problem’s Driveways section.

DO NOT label driveways with either curb ramp or missing curb ramp labels, because pedestrians are not meant to follow the driveway into the street.

Small side streets or alleyways

DO place a curb ramp or missing curb ramp label if the side street is not level with the sidewalk, since a curb ramp is required for the street to be passable. (ADA 402.2).

DO NOT label with a curb ramp or missing curb ramp if the side street is level with the sidewalk, since the intersection is passable without a curb ramp. (ADA 402.2).

Crosswalks

DO place curb ramp or missing curb ramp labels anywhere there is a crosswalk (as long as there is a sidewalk as well), as this implies that pedestrians are meant to cross (but do not mistake stop lines for crosswalks, as in image on the right!) (ADA 303.4).

There is curb ramp on opposite side of street

DO place a missing curb ramp label if one side of the street has a curb ramp but the opposite side does not, since the presence of one curb ramp implies that this is meant to be part of a pedestrian route. Normally this would get a severity of 5, but in the image on the right, there is an adjacent driveway that can be used in place of a curb ramp (ADA 303.4), so it gets a severity of 4.

Islands

DO place curb ramp or missing curb ramp label(s) if the island interferes with the crosswalk, as in the image on the right. (ADA 406.7).

DO NOT place curb ramp or missing curb ramp labels if the island is cut (as in leftmost illustration, below) or does not interfere with or block the crosswalk.

For islands that should have a curb ramp but do not, severity of the problem depends on how far into traffic one would have to go to avoid the island.

Extension of walkway to a house

DO place a curb ramp label only if there is a curb ramp present, or a missing curb ramp label only if there is a curb ramp on the opposite side of the street.

DO NOT place a missing curb ramp label if there is no curb ramp on either side of the street, as curb ramps are generally not expected here because it is not at an intersection (ADA 406.6).

Friction strips

If a curb ramp does not have friction strips, then it should have a severity > 1. If a missing friction strip is the only problem with a ramp, then the severity would just be 2 (ADA 705). In the example on the left, the severity would be 2. The severity would be 1 for those on the right, since they have friction strips, and meet all other requirements.

Surface problems ON curb ramps

If a curb ramp has surface problems, increase the severity of the curb ramp, but DO NOT place a surface problem label. The rationale is that cracks and such in a curb ramp are one of the many factors that can make a curb ramp less passable, so there should be a higher curb ramp severity. But we don’t need to double-count the issue by also labeling it as a surface problem.

NOTE: This differs from how we deal with obstacles on curb ramps. We actually take the opposite approach with obstacles. The idea is that a bad curb ramp is just a bad curb ramp, while an obstacle is something external to the curb ramp. This is honestly pretty confusing and could be clarified in the future.

Severity ratings should be equivalent to how they are done for surface problems, described in the sections on Sidewalk Cracks and Grass between sidewalk blocks. However, here are a few examples.

Narrow ramps

If a curb ramp appears narrow, then it should have severity > 1 (the ADA requires a width of at least 3 ft - ADA 403.5.1). Since we have no measuring tools, use your best judgement. For example, the ramp on the right would get a severity of 3 because it is a bit narrower than 3ft, and is also missing a friction strip.

Debris on/around ramp

If a curb ramp has a large amount of dead leaves (or other debris) on it, the severity should be > 1 (FHWA Guide for Maintaining Pedestrian Facilities for Enhanced Safety 3.2.2), but would not result in severity > 3 if the debris is the only issue. This could cause slippery conditions or could slow down wheelchair users in extreme cases. Do not place a surface problem label along with the curb ramp label. Only place a surface problem label on the sidewalk (not on curb ramp).

Insufficient landing space

If a curb ramp does not have enough landing space at the top of the ramp for making turns, the severity should be > 1 (the ADA requires at least 3ft - ADA 406.4). In situations of insufficient landing space, severity also depends on presence and steepness of the angled sides of a ramp (called flares), so also consult the section on Steep flares. The example on the right gets a severity of ~2, since there is not enough landing space, but the flares are not too steep. The ramps on the 2nd row would get a severity of ~3, since they both have no landing space at all, while again having flared sides that are not steep.

Steep flares

If a curb ramp has overly steep angled sides (called flares), severity should be > 1, but would not result in severity > 3 if the flares are the only issue. Steep flares are particularly problematic when there is Insufficient landing space at the top of the ramp, since wheelchair users may need to use the flares to make turns and could lose their balance. However, steep flares are not an issue when pedestrians are not meant to be using them anyway, as in the rightmost illustration.

The leftmost image has very steep sides, is far too narrow, and also is missing a friction strip, so it would get a severity of ~3. The middle image has insufficient landing space, but it’s flares are not steep, so the severity is only ~2. The illustration on the right does not need flares, since the sides of the ramp are lined by grass (meaning pedestrians are not meant to use it), so the severity would probably be 1.

Steep counter slopes

If a curb ramp has a steep counter slope (the slope opposite to the end of a ramp, see left illustration below) then the severity of the curb ramp should be > 1 (the ADA requires counter slopes to be at no more than a ~3 degree angle - ADA 406.2). You only need to consider obvious cases of steep counter slopes, as we have no angle measurement tools. The illustration on the left would get a severity of ~3 for the curb ramp, assuming the rest of the ADA standards are met.

Pooled water

If a curb ramp seems to have pooled water due to poor drainage, severity should be > 1 (ADA 405.10), but would not result in a severity of 5 if pooled water is the only issue (since water typically pools only after rainfall).
